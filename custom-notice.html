What is TOR?

The Tor network is run by some 5000 volunteers who use the free software provided by the Tor Project to run Tor routers. Client connections are routed through multiple relays, and are multiplexed together on the connections between relays. The system does not record logs of client connections or previous hops.

This is because the Tor network is a censorship resistance, privacy, and anonymity system used by whistle blowers, journalists, Chinese dissidents skirting the Great Firewall, abuse victims, stalker targets, the US military, and law enforcement, just to name a few.

Unfortunately, some people misuse the network. However, compared to the rate of legitimate use (the IP range in question processes nearly a gigabit of traffic per second), abuse complaints are rare.

Why May Administrators See This IP in Their Logs?

If you are an administrator or run a website, you may see the IP address of a TOR exit relay in your server logs when users access your website through the TOR network. This happens because the last node in the TOR network, known as the exit relay, is responsible for sending the user's request to the intended website on the regular internet. As a result, your website's server logs will record the IP address of the TOR exit relay, not the original user's IP address.
It is essential to understand that while the IP address in the logs belongs to the TOR exit relay, the actual user remains anonymous as their original IP address is hidden behind multiple layers of encryption and relay nodes.

Why Administrators Shouldn't Block TOR Exit Node IPs

Blocking TOR exit node IPs might seem like a tempting solution to prevent potential abuse or security threats, but it can have unintended consequences and hinder legitimate user access. Here are some key reasons why administrators should reconsider blocking TOR exit node IPs:

Preserving User Anonymity:
TOR is designed to provide online anonymity for its users. By blocking TOR exit node IPs, administrators may inadvertently deny access to users who rely on TOR for genuine privacy and security concerns. People in repressive regimes, journalists, whistleblowers, and activists often use TOR to access information safely and communicate without fear of surveillance.

Maintaining Internet Freedom:
Embracing TOR and allowing its users access fosters an open and free internet. Blocking TOR exit nodes could be seen as an attempt to restrict the flow of information and impede the principles of a decentralized and open web.

What can administrators do instead of blocking this exit relay?

The Tor Project provides an automated DNSRBL for you to query to flag posts coming from Tor nodes as requiring special review. You can also use this DNSRBL to only allow Tor IPs to read but not post comments.

However, be aware that this may be just one jerk amongst many legitimate Tor users who use your forums. You might have luck getting rid of this jerk by temporarily limiting account creation to require Gmail accounts before posting, or by requiring account creation be done over non-Tor before posting.

In general, we believe that problems like this are best solved by improving your service to defend against the attack from the Internet at large. Brute force login attempts can be reduced/slowed by Captchas, which is the approach taken by Gmail for this same problem. In fact, Google provides a free Captcha service, complete with code for easy inclusion in a number of systems to help other sites deal with this issue.
